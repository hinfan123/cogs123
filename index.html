Overview

Team Members: Bradley Arca, Winson Dieu, Joseph Lee, Hien Phan, Adam Yu

It’s ironic how it’s more difficult to find friends in today’s day and age when we are historically more connected then we have ever been. AFriend was created as a social computing system to explore how might we leverage geolocation and anonymity to create an opportunity for strangers to become AFriend to each other.

We primarily prototyped AFriend at UC San Diego leveraging our library as a hotspot to have data-driven designs.

Problem Space

The major need we identified was the barrier of establishing genuine connection and the fear of social repercussions associated with being vulnerable.

Direct Observations

We spent 2 hrs+ into Geisel library to observe 300+ people, approximately 15% to 25% of students there were on their phones were counted as potential users.

Surveys

We discovered that 6/10 users do not take online interactions seriously and that the anonymity aspect addresses the fear of social reprecussions so people could unapologetically be themselves without over commitment.

Primary Interviews

We discovered that 6/10 users do not take online interactions seriously and that the anonymity aspect addresses the fear of social reprecussions so people could unapologetically be themselves without over commitment.

7/10 interviewees like the aspect of starting of anonymous and then transistioning to revealing themselves if the conversations went well.

One of our interviewees asked “How long would it take for people to go the next step and introduce each other, and meeting the star person and see who that actually goes.”

This ended up defining our the key painpoint we were addressing in why’s difficult to make friends.

Ideation

Used the concept of localized chat based on the geo-location of the area. By keeping emphasis on the aspect of anonymity, we wanted to match anonymous users based on their interest.

Ideas from creating an algorithm that matches people based on common interest to implementing the Big 5 personality traits.

In regards to private chats, developed “wave” feature to notify an anonymous users to request a private chat, and “roll a dice” to be matched with users based on the higher percentage rate based on interest.

Prototyping

Prototype

We used discord to represent the online messaging features of our prototype and we discovered that the features discord has would mesh really well with what a final product of our application would be.

However, since we didn't moderate the chats carefully enough, content that people could find offensive did slip into the conversations.

In addition to this, we noticed that the audience did not move around to change rooms, so future iterations might include things that incentivize moving from one location to another.

Final Designs

Co-located Space

Each chat room would be based on a physical location. Users may only join nearby locations to encourage interaction online and potentially offline.

Range

Users would able to see chatrooms and activity within a certain range, but cannot participate until they are within a smaller range dependent on the location. Incentivizes users to move to locations and be in the same physical space as other users, and discourage randoms from “trolling” or harassment.

Chatrooms

Each chat room would be based on a physical location. Users may only join nearby locations to encourage interaction online and potentially create opportunities to meet offline.

Messaging

Individual chats would only between 2 people and would allow for a more intimate conversation. Users would indicate in their preferences whether or not they would allow the system to automatically match them users with similar interests.

What We Learned

Geolocation was a good stepping stone that allows users to interact with people synchronously in a co-located space.

Our messaging had difficulties as we found that it’s difficult to moderate what goes on in the group chats and can turn away a lot of potential users.

Best use cases for us to pivot towards would be for users to use this application during class to help each other with notes / class updates and create study groups based off of the group chats or individual/private ones.

Takeaway

Our key takeaway with our live prototype that was tested on ~55 participants was: 60% of them were receptive to AFriend and to address the potential edge cases

  • Feeling comfortable within an open chat forum.
  • Addressing potential toxicity that arises in similar products
  • Anonymity is a double-edged sword as it allows people to create meaningful discourse, but it can also create negative environments in which users would not want to be a part of.
